## The problem

Max-Cut is deceptively simple to state: given a graph $G = (V, E)$, partition the vertices into two sets to maximize the number of edges that cross the partition. It's NP-hard in general, which means we don't expect to solve it exactly in polynomial time. The interesting question is: how well can we approximate it?

The integer formulation assigns a label $x_i \in \{-1, +1\}$ to each vertex; an edge $\{i,j\}$ is cut when $x_i \neq x_j$, i.e. when $x_i x_j = -1$:

$$\max_{x \in \{-1,+1\}^n} \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} \frac{1 - x_i x_j}{2}$$

Trivially, a random partition gives you an expected cut of half the edges: a 0.5-approximation. The Goemans-Williamson algorithm from 1995 does substantially better: it achieves an approximation ratio of approximately 0.878. The key insight is to relax the integer problem to a continuous one — each vertex gets a unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ instead of a scalar in $\{-1,+1\}$ — solve the resulting semidefinite program, and then round the solution back to integers via a random hyperplane.

## Why SDP is the right tool here

Semidefinite programs are a generalization of linear programs where the variables are symmetric matrices constrained to be positive semidefinite. They're solvable in polynomial time, and they provide tighter relaxations for many combinatorial problems than LP relaxations do, which is exactly what you need when you're trying to prove approximation guarantees.

The geometry is what makes this satisfying. The SDP relaxation replaces each scalar $x_i$ with a unit vector $\mathbf{v}_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and the objective becomes:

$$\max \sum_{\{i,j\} \in E} \frac{1 - \langle \mathbf{v}_i,\, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle}{2}, \qquad \|\mathbf{v}_i\| = 1$$

When you embed a $\{-1,+1\}$ solution as $\pm 1$-vectors, the SDP objective exactly matches the cut value. But the relaxation also allows vector configurations that no $\{-1,+1\}$ assignment can achieve. That's where the gap comes from.

## The Unique Games Conjecture and tightness

The ratio 0.878 isn't arbitrary. It comes directly from the geometry of Gaussian rounding. What Goemans and Williamson proved is that the rounding loses at most a factor of $\alpha_{\text{GW}} \approx 0.878$ relative to the SDP optimum, where:

$$\alpha_{\text{GW}} = \min_{0 \leq \theta \leq \pi} \frac{2\theta}{\pi\,(1 - \cos\theta)} \approx 0.878$$

The deeper question, "is this tight? Can you do better?", turns out to connect to one of the central open problems in complexity theory.

Khot's Unique Games Conjecture (2002) implies that the Goemans-Williamson ratio is optimal: no polynomial-time algorithm can do better unless the conjecture is false.  It's a fascinating case where a conjectural hardness result shapes the design space for algorithms, before anyone has proved the conjecture.

## What this kind of work teaches you

Working on approximation algorithms taught me something I hadn't expected: that the interesting question is often not "can we solve this?" but "how much can we know about the solution without solving it?" The SDP relaxation doesn't solve Max-Cut; it gives you a certificate, a structured lower bound on how much you'd lose by rounding. Learning to think in terms of certificates and gaps changed how I approach other problems.
